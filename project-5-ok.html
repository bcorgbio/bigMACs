Introduction

Maintaining a stable body temperature is an important physiological role for humans, and thermoregulatory defenses keep the body in consistent temperatures. Examples of thermoregulatory defenses include shivering or sweating, which are responses invoked by the hypothalamus in an attempt to keep the body temperature steady under changing conditions using Campbell (2008). The purpose of this study is to measure body temperatures of 4 different people under varying activity levels using the DHT11 sensor. After collecting this data, it was examined using a ggplot from R studio. The data from all subjects were combined into one table, and showed the standard deviations and means, and the temperature measurements for each activity level for each subject.

Methods

In order to evaluate the effect of physical activity on body temperature, we recorded the axillary temperature (armpit) after a duration of inactivity and intense activity. We recorded these temperatures using a DHT11 sensor collected on Arduino software. The data from the four group members were then compiled into 8 different csvs detailing their active and inactive body temperatures.

The inactivity data for each member was taken on the couch for 100 seconds or until the temperature seemed to stabilize. Comparetively, for the activation period we all participated in the game, Just Dance, for five minutes, and then sat down to measure the new body temperature for 100 seconds. We then plotted each members temperature using a +/- standard deviation to compare the means of all of our temperatures.

Results

Figure 1: Boxplots of body temperature data across all participants (Jack, John, Philip, Samantha) and activity types (after sitting on couch and after playing JustDance). Means are labelled within plots using a hollow rhombus

When we compare the interpersonal mean and median body temperatures after sitting on the couch versus after playing JustDance, we observe a difference of no more than 1C. Since the confidence intervals also remain relatively stable and overlapped, we can conclude that there was not a significant change in body temperature due to physical activity. Any slight deviations in body temperature is more likely due to random and experimental errors.

Discussion

Based on a study done by Satinoff, Kent, and Hurd (1991), mean female rat body temperatures measured via telemetry increased from 36.0C to 36.5C during the day and 38.1C to 39.5C during the night when housed in activity wheels over the course of a two week cycle. Their data is both consistent with our data in that there were no large changes to body temperature and inconsistent in that we did not observe a positive correlation between activity and increasing mean or median body temperature. In fact, our collected median body temperatures, which are less affected by outliers than mean body temperatures, also saw decreases when moving from post-couch to post-JustDance. Although we can say that endotherms, like rats and humans, do not suffer from significant changes in body temperature due to an inherent thermoregulation system, further work should be done to account for other factors, such as intensity of activity, duration of activity, and length of sampling (a day versus over cycle of weeks).
